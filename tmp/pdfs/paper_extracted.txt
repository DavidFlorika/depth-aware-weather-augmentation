A Depth-Aware, Physics-Based Data Augmentation Framework for
Simulating Rain and Fog Distortions in Outdoor RGB Images
Rongxuan Duan
Pioneer Research Institute
Abstract
Outdoor RGB driving datasets often underrepresent adverse-weather conditions such as rain and fog, making
it difficult to train distortion-aware detection models and evaluate robustness systematically. This paper presents a
depth-aware, physics-based augmentation framework for simulating rain and fog in outdoor driving images. Using
BDD100K as the baseline dataset, the framework generates rainy images through image-plane rendering, depth-aware
physics-based rendering using depth maps produced by Depth Anything V2, Cycle-GAN translation, and a hybrid
Cycle-GAN plus physics-based pipeline. For fog simulation, a Cycle-GAN generator trained on Cityscapes and Foggy
Cityscapes is applied to BDD100K while preserving object-detection labels. The augmented datasets are evaluated by
training YOLOv8n detectors and testing them on clear, rainy, and foggy BDD100K validation splits. Results show
that replacing clean training data with augmented data generally reduces clear-weather and rainy-weather detection
performance, whereas combining clean and augmented data preserves performance close to the clean-data baseline.
The strongest gains are observed on the foggy split, although this finding remains preliminary due to its limited
foggy sample size. Overall, the framework shows that controlled adverse-weather augmentation is most effective as a
supplement to clean training data and provides a practical foundation for future robustness studies.
1 Introduction
Images used in computer vision are often distorted. Distortion in computer vision refers to any signal that produces
a visual deviation in an image/video from its expected appearance (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al., n.d.). When the
original image/video is unavailable, Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al. (n.d.) also consider “observed visual comfort or
simply annoying effect on the aesthetic visual appearance” to be a distortion. These distortions can be sorted into
In-Capture Distortion, which occurs during the initial acquisition phase, including physical artifacts like blur, noise,
uneven illumination, color shifts, haze, rain, snow, and other visibility changes; and Post-Capture Distortion, which
refers to image deformation or degradation that occurs after images are captured, such as transmission impairments,
enhancement artifacts, and adversarial distortion.
Although these effects may appear minor to human observers, they can change the statistical structure of an image
and reduce the reliability of machine learning models trained primarily on clean data (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al.,
n.d.). Distortions affect the performance of high-level tasks, such as object classification, object detection, and scene
understanding (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al., n.d.). High-level tasks are more sensitive to distortions than low-level
tasks, such as edge detection, image enhancement, and image colorization, because models often learn specific, localized
semantic features rather than global context. Therefore, distortion detection and removal are possible preprocessing
steps for high-level tasks to decrease the impact on performance. Figure 1 demonstrates several common distortions in
high-level tasks.
For machine learning, distortion is not only an image-quality issue but is also affected by the dataset and training.
Models usually learn from the distribution of inputs they are given, so a model trained only on clean images may have
poor generalizability when processing the same object appearing under poor visibility, weather effects, or degraded
contrast. Distortion-specific datasets are important because they make it possible to evaluate model robustness by
distortion type and severity, rather than reporting only a single clean-data accuracy score (Muhammad Ali Qureshi
et al., n.d.).

Current datasets of natural distortions have important limitations. Real rain, haze, fog, and snow images can be
difficult to collect at scale, and the resulting data may be unbalanced across weather type, severity, scene category, and
annotation quality. Many benchmark datasets also include distortions as broad corruptions rather than as physically
meaningful, scene-dependent effects. This limits how well they can support controlled robustness studies on deep
learning models’ performance under different types of distortions or targeted training strategies, which introduce
controlled distortions to train models for more stable performance when facing distortions.
Related work has explored both distortion recognition and natural-distortion simulation. Survey work on distor-
tion classification emphasizes that multiple and natural distortions remain difficult to categorize and model consis-
tently (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al., n.d.). In weather-focused vision, rain simulation has been used to render controlled
rain effects for robustness evaluation, while haze and fog modeling often relies on atmospheric image-formation ideas
such as scattering, attenuation, and reduced scene contrast. Work on adverse-weather perception, including foggy
driving scenes, also shows that real weather can strongly affect downstream recognition and detection performance.
My work uses a distortion augmentation framework, which adds controlled rain and fog effects to an existing
baseline dataset, BDD100K (Wolfe, n.d.). The framework is evaluated by comparing the performance of a downstream
object detection model, YOLOv8n (“Explore Ultralytics YOLOv8,” 2023; “What Is YOLOv8: An In-Depth Exploration
of the Internal Features of the Next-Generation Object Detector,” n.d.), on the original dataset and the augmented
dataset.

create rainy- or foggy-looking images from clear images, while the discriminator D tries to distinguish between real

In weather augmentation, Cycle-GAN is useful because it generates realistic rainy or foggy images from clear

et al., 2020). Therefore, this work uses Cycle-GAN to generate rainy and foggy images, but it also uses a physics-based,
depth-aware renderer to generate rain with controllable appearance.
3 Related Work
3.1 Distortion Classification and Evaluation
Traditional distortion classification and evaluation models utilize blur indices, noise estimators, natural scene statistics,
image quality metrics for distortion severity evaluation, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and K-Nearest Neighbors
(KNN) for distortion classification (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al., n.d.). Recent distortion classification and detection
models use Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and temporal models. Ahn et al. (2017) train a Y-shaped CNN
for joint distortion classification and localization. Other methods for distortion classification include feature pooling,
relating metrics with specific distortions, and Natural Scene Statistics (NSS) (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al., n.d.).
These methods support learned distortion features, yet most datasets focus on controlled distortions rather than natural
weather.
The quality of visual content in images and distortion severity are important factors that affect performance in high-
level tasks, including object classification, object detection, and scene understanding. Using distortion classification and
detection models could help researchers fine-tune models that are more resistant to distortions. However, my work
helps fine-tune models by presenting a data augmentation framework that increases models’ resilience to rain and fog
distortions during training and does not require a distortion detection and removal process.
3.2 Current Limitations of Distortion
Datasets
Current distortion datasets give labels and severity levels, but many examples come from synthetic, single-type, quality-
assessment settings. LIVE, TID2008, TID2013, CSIQ, Waterloo, KADID-10K, LIVEMD, and MDID help keep a
controlled evaluation. However, they do not contain natural weather with precise physical labels. Rain, haze, fog, and
snow depend on lighting, exposure, particle density, scene depth, and occlusion. There is a lack of labeled, controlled
multi-distortion datasets. Consequently, classification and localization of multiple distortions remain challenging due
to a lack of labeled, controlled datasets (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al., n.d.; Tremblay et al., 2021). My work adds
controlled, artificial distortions, which addresses the lack of labeled, controlled datasets, especially for outdoor datasets.
3.3 Traditional Rain Rendering
Garg and Nayar model rain streak photometry and spatio-temporal behavior. Their work links rain visibility to drop size,
velocity, exposure time, scene radiance, camera settings, and drop shape oscillation. This result matters for rendering
because a transparent 2D texture ignores camera and scene factors. Traditional rain rendering lacks the effect of moisture
on the scene, and it often appears to be unrealistic. For instance, traditional rain rendering lacks wet surfaces such as
water on the ground, and it creates raindrops that are supposed to be hidden behind buildings (Tremblay et al., 2021).
However, this framework supported rain detection, removal, rendering, and camera-based rain measurement (Garg &
Nayar, 2007).
Figure 3 exemplifies three traditional rain rendering methods: rain100H, rain800, and did-MDN (Tremblay et al.,
2021).

Tremblay et al. (2021) compare three rendering routes: physics-based, data-driven, and hybrid. They classify

located far away from the camera and appears as fog. The physics-based route combines fog-like rain, rain particles,
and photometric modeling. The renderer estimates outdoor illumination because a raindrop receives light from a wider
angular range (approximately 160 degrees) than the camera’s view (70 to 100 degrees). This design improves physical
control over a flat overlay. The same design also depends on sky visibility, environmental lighting or photometry
symmetry, and compute cost (Hold-Geoffroy et al., 2018; Tremblay et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2019).
The data-driven route uses a GAN-style image translation model. A GAN trains two networks: a generator
synthesizes target-domain images, and a discriminator judges whether each image looks real or generated. Adversarial
training on the generator outputs rainy-looking images. The output from the generator contains features such as dark
sky, rainy environment, wet surfaces, and dim illumination. However, GAN is less controllable, which risks rain density,
depth ordering, and scene consistency. GAN’s focus on global appearance also creates unrealistic features, such as fake
water reflections (Huang et al., 2018; Isola et al., 2018; Tremblay et al., 2021).
Tremblay et al. (2021) render rain over KITTI, Cityscapes, and nuScenes. They evaluate object detection, semantic
segmentation, and depth estimation under increasing degradation. The paper reports degraded performance as rain
intensity increases and more robust performance after fine-tuning using rain-augmented datasets. The paper reports that
outputs from GAN only are user-rated as the most realistic images; outputs from PBR plus GAN perform best for data
augmentation (Tremblay et al., 2021).
My work is inspired by the framework from Tremblay et al. My work uses the traditional rain rendering framework
with depth estimation to build controlled, depth-dependent rain streaks; my work also involves using a Cycle-GAN
model that adds rainy and foggy visual effects.
3.5 Fog Rendering
Fog rendering often follows the atmospheric image formation model. Scene radiance attenuates with distance, while
airlight grows along the camera’s ray. Sakaridis et al. (2018) apply this model to clear Cityscapes images and produce
Foggy Cityscapes. Using depth makes fog stronger in far regions rather than applying it uniformly across the image.
Training with synthetic fog improves semantic foggy scene understanding, but results depend on depth quality, density
choices, and the synthetic-to-real gap (Sakaridis et al., 2018).
Clear2Fog extends this direction with monocular depth estimation and atmospheric light estimation. Monocular
depth estimation does not require 3D constructions such as LiDAR; rather, this paper uses Apple Depth Pro to produce
an estimated depth map. The pipeline simulates fog on clear-weather driving datasets and tries to preserve camera-
LiDAR consistency. Without relying on LiDAR, Clear2Fog remains stable under extreme conditions like fog, snow or
haze. Bijelic et al. (2020) focus on multimodal perception rather than rendering. The dataset and fusion model show
different failure patterns across camera, LiDAR, radar, and gated near-infrared sensors (Bijelic et al., 2020; Mohamed
& Huang, 2026).

3.6 Depth Estimation and Application in Distortion Rendering
Depth provides the geometry needed for natural distortion placement. In haze and fog, depth sets attenuation and
airlight strength. In rain, depth helps determine the severity, intensity and placement of rain streaks. A renderer without
geometry often places plausible-looking weather in physically inconsistent locations (Sakaridis et al., 2018; Tremblay
et al., 2021).
Depth estimation models estimate distance from an image or complete sparse sensor-depth data. The output supplies
an approximate geometry map when a dataset lacks dense ground truth. Jaritz et al. study sparse and dense depth
completion for driving scenes. Depth Anything V2 offers a modern monocular option trained with synthetic images and
pseudo-labeled real images. This makes Depth Anything V2 useful for RGB-only distortion rendering (Jaritz et al.,
2018; Yang et al., 2024).
My work uses the depth map output from Depth Anything V2 as the guiding parameter for the transparency and
appearance of rain streaks and the translucency and intensity of fog. The depth-map output from Depth Anything V2 is
stable and reliable, and it is a strong model for completing my computer vision tasks.
3.7 Datasets With Distortions
General distortion and corruption datasets support baselines, but natural weather rendering requires more geometry and
scene context. The distortion survey lists LIVE, TID2008, TID2013, CSIQ, Waterloo, KADID-10K, LIVEMD, and
MDID as major image-quality or multi-distortion resources. ImageNet-C standardizes common corruption robustness
for image classifiers. These resources help robustness framing. They do not model depth-aware rain, haze, or
fog (Muhammad Ali Qureshi et al., n.d.).
Weather-specific datasets match the project more closely. ACDC includes fog, nighttime, rain, and snow with
dense driving-scene annotations and normal-condition correspondences for many scenes. Foggy Cityscapes and
rain-augmented KITTI, Cityscapes, and nuScenes preserve existing labels while adding synthetic weather. The
limitation of these datasets is the lack of controlled severity or intensity, depth-aware labels, and controlled natural
distortions (Sakaridis et al., 2018, 2021; Tremblay et al., 2021).
3.8 BDD100K
BDD100K serves as a baseline dataset for my work, because the data are large, diverse, and driving-focused. The
dataset contains 100,000 driving videos and supports detection, segmentation, lane marking, drivable area, and related
driving labels. Figure 4 shows three images with various weather labels from the BDD100K dataset. The BDD100K
dataset also includes geographic, environmental, and weather variation. The limitations are missing paired clean/adverse
views and controlled severity labels. A depth-aware rain and fog renderer would turn BDD100K into a controlled
robustness testbed while keeping real driving diversity (Yu et al., 2018).

4 Methodology
Data augmentation is a strategy for improving computer vision models’ performance by artificially increasing the
diversity of training images through transformations such as cropping, rotating, flipping and adding noise. These
methods are especially useful when access to labeled image data is limited, because they expose a model to variations


===== PAGE 7 =====

7
it may encounter in real-world visual tasks. In computer vision, data augmentation helps image classifiers learn
features that are less dependent on a single viewpoint, orientation, or image style, reducing overfitting and improving
generalization (Perez & Wang, 2017).
Data augmentation is the primary method to increase a dataset’s size and variety. In the context of this paper, it refers
to adding rain and fog to images. As discussed in previous sections, collecting data with various natural distortions
for an outdoor dataset is challenging. Therefore, using data augmentation to render natural distortions is expected to
improve performance of deep learning models. My work focuses on rain and fog distortions in outdoor RGB images.
4.1 Rain Rendering
Rain appears as dynamic-looking particles and brings a rainy effect to the image. Inspired by Tremblay et al. (Tremblay
et al., 2021), my physics-based rain rendering method that simulates raindrops builds on depth estimation and uses a
Cycle-GAN model that creates rainy effects.
4.1.1 Simple Rain Rendering
Simple rain rendering is used as a baseline physics-based augmentation method. This method does not use a depth map
or estimate the 3D position of objects in the scene. Instead, it adds rain directly in the 2D image plane. The purpose of
this method is to create a controllable comparison point for evaluating whether depth information improves the realism
and usefulness of rain augmentation.
Given an input RGB/BGR image I with height H and width W , the simple rain renderer first defines an image area
scale A = HW/1000 and a minimum image dimension m = min(H, W ). A severity value s controls the strength of
the rain. In the simple rain rendering setting, three severities (“light”, “medium”, “heavy”) refer to three fixed severity
values, each determining the number of rain streaks, the streak length, the streak width, opacity, etc. Higher severity
values produce denser rain, longer motion trails, and a darker rainy appearance.
The renderer creates rain using three 2D streak layers. These layers represent different visual scales of rain: a
layer of longer and thicker streaks, a layer of medium streaks, and a layer of shorter fine streaks. Each layer l has a
streak count N , streak length L , streak width W , base opacity α , brightness coefficient ϵ , and shadow suppression

4.1.2 Depth-Aware Physics-Based Rain Rendering
To generate synthetic raindrop appearance with scene geometry, the depth-aware physics-based rain rendering (PBR)
method combines image-space rain streak synthesis, atmospheric scattering, and linear-light compositing, based on the
monocular depth map produced by Depth Anything V2 (Yang et al., 2024). The objective of this renderer is to produce
controllable raindrops while remaining consistent with scene geometry and depth.

4.1.3 Rain Cycle-GAN
While the physics-based renderer simulates raindrops and fine mist, it lacks rainy characteristics such as wetness,
reflections, sky, and lighting. Scenes generated from the physics-based renderer may have realistic raindrops and mist,
but they lack an overall rainy look. These characteristics are hard to generate through a controlled method, whereas
Cycle-GAN can handle image-to-image translation tasks.
The dataset used for training is BDD100K. It contains seven labels: “clear”, “overcast”, “undefined”, “snowy”,
“rainy”, “partly cloudy” and “foggy”. The corresponding numbers of images in the training split are 37344, 8700, 8119,
5549, 5070, 5881, 130, respectively; the corresponding numbers of images in the validation split are 5346, 1239, 1157,
769, 738, 738, 13, respectively. Labels “clear” and “rainy” are used for training, which refer to clear weather and rainy
weather, respectively.

4.1.4 Framework Combining PBR and Rain Cycle-GAN
The final rain augmentation framework combines the Rain Cycle-GAN model and the depth-aware physics-based
renderer into a hybrid pipeline. The purpose is to use the strengths of both methods. Rain Cycle-GAN generates the
overall rainy appearance, while the physics-based rain renderer adds controllable rainfall, including rain streaks, fine
mist, and depth-aware visibility.
The pipeline starts with a clear-weather image from BDD100K. First, the image is passed through the trained Rain
Cycle-GAN generator to produce a rainy-looking output of the same scene. The output preserves structures such as
road layout, vehicles, buildings, and other structures; it shifts the image toward the rainy domain. Depth Anything V2
also estimates a monocular depth map from the input image, which is used by the PBR renderer.
After the Cycle-GAN translation, the PBR renderer composites rain onto the GAN-generated image. The severity
parameter controls the number and appearance of rain streaks. Near rain layers contain larger and more visible streaks,

4.2 Fog Augmentation
Foggy images usually contain bright skies, reduced contrast, desaturated colors, softened distant objects, and lower
visibility across the scene. Although fog can be simulated with physics-based atmospheric scattering models (ASM),
realistic fog depends on depth, illumination, atmospheric light, and camera exposure (Cordts et al., 2016). Simple
brightness or color transformations are therefore not enough to produce convincing foggy driving scenes. In this work, I
use Cycle-GAN for fog augmentation because it learns the overall visual style of fog from data while approximately
preserving the road layout, vehicles, buildings, and other scene structures from the input image.
BDD100K contains very few foggy images: there are only 130 foggy images in the training split and 13 in the
validation split. Since this is too small for stable, robust Cycle-GAN training, I trained the Fog Cycle-GAN model using
the Cityscapes clear images as domain X and Foggy Cityscapes as domain Y (Cordts et al., 2016; Sakaridis et al., 2018).
The Foggy Cityscapes dataset is synthetically generated from the clean Cityscapes images for computer vision training,
using the Atmospheric Scattering Model (ASM), which blends clear-weather images with a uniform atmospheric light
based on scene depth and a fog density coefficient (Cordts et al., 2016). It supports more consistent and stable training
for my Fog Cycle-GAN model because it contains urban driving images similar to the foggy BDD100K subset.
The Fog Cycle-GAN follows the same unpaired image-to-image translation framework as the Rain Cycle-GAN (Zhu
et al., 2020). Domain X contains clear Cityscapes images, and domain Y contains Foggy Cityscapes images. A fog
density of 0.02 refers to how quickly visibility decreases over distance. This density corresponds to a visibility range of
around 150 meters, and it is the densest fog density in the Foggy Cityscapes dataset (Cordts et al., 2016). It is chosen to
create a more distinct difference between domain X (the clear images) and domain Y (the foggy images). The training
dataset contains 3000 clear images and 3000 foggy images, with 400 clear and 400 foggy images used for testing.
Images are loaded at width 512 and randomly cropped to 384 during training. The model is trained using a batch size of
1, a learning rate of 0.0001, an identity loss weight of 1.0, no horizontal flipping, 10 epochs with constant learning rate,
and 10 decaying epochs.
After training, the clear-to-fog generator is applied to the same 3000 clear images from the BDD100K dataset used
in rain augmentation, generating 3000 clear-foggy image pairs. The generated outputs are resized back to the original
BDD100K image dimensions and saved as fog-augmented training images. Since Cycle-GAN changes the visual
appearance but preserves the main scene layout, the original BDD100K object-detection labels are reused and exported
in YOLOv8 format. This produces a fog-augmented BDD100K training set with 3000 images and corresponding
bounding-box labels.
The Fog Cycle-GAN outputs preserve input structures, and they contain most foggy features, such as washed-out
skies, reduced contrast, desaturated colors, softened distant objects, and lower visibility, as shown in Figure 10.

Average Precision (AP) is computed from the precision-recall curve for each object class by sorting predictions
by confidence score and measuring how precision and recall change as more predictions are accepted (Wang et

moderate localization requirement, while mAP50–95 is stricter as higher IoU thresholds require the predicted bounding

the Internal Features of the Next-Generation Object Detector,” n.d.). These metrics allow the experiment to compare
YOLOv8n detection performance from varied points of view, following mAP-based object detection evaluation used in
established works such as Tremblay et al. (2021) and Mohamed and Huang (2026).
5.2 Results
To evaluate whether the proposed augmentation framework improves object detection robustness, YOLOv8n was
trained with different versions of the BDD100K training data and tested on clear, rainy, and foggy validation splits.
The training images are resized from 1280 × 720 to 640 × 640. The training datasets include a clean baseline dataset
containing 3,000 images, augmented-image-only datasets obtained by applying simple rain, PBR, Rain Cycle-GAN,
PBR plus GAN, and fog augmentation on the same 3,000 images in the clean baseline dataset, and datasets containing
3,000 augmented images combined with 3,000 clear images. The main metric is mAP50–95, with precision, recall, and
mAP50 reported for additional context. The clear validation split contains 5,346 images, the rainy split contains 738
images, and the foggy split contains 13 images. Note that the foggy validation split contains very few images, which
requires extra caution when drawing conclusions.

5.3 Discussion and Limitations
YOLOv8n models trained with the augmented-training methods are expected to outperform the model trained with the
baseline method; however, the clear-baseline training method performs best in clear-image and rainy-image evaluations.
Several reasons could potentially contribute to this failure.
First, the training process uses transfer learning. YOLOv8n is initialized from pretrained weights that were already
learned from large-scale natural image datasets. These large-scale natural image datasets likely contain adverse-weather
images, so the clean baseline model may already generalize reasonably well to mild rainy and foggy validation images
without needing synthetic weather augmentation. When the model is fine-tuned on augmented images, especially
synthetic images that may contain artifacts or domain gaps, the training process may decrease the model’s ability
to generalize well across all features and maintain a stable performance under adverse conditions. In this case,
augmentation may not fail because weather simulation is useless, but because the pretrained model already provides a
strong starting point and adding augmented data to the training dataset may not benefit the model overall, as it introduces
a domain shift.
Second, the Rain and Fog Cycle-GAN models are trained using images cropped at size 384 × 384, which is small
compared with the original BDD100K size 1280 × 720 and the original Cityscapes size 2048 × 1024. Despite speeding


===== PAGE 18 =====

18
up the training process, a small crop size sacrifices stability and performance. The trained Cycle-GAN models are
unstable: the presence of white random noise and the unrealistic appearance of rain and fog in the augmented datasets
negatively affect the performance of the YOLOv8n model trained on them.
Third, as discussed in Section 4.1.3, the BDD100K rainy images are mostly captured post-rainfall; therefore, the
differences between rainy and clear images appear to be minor. Accordingly, the Rain Cycle-GAN generator trained on
these post-rainfall images may be unable to capture real rain features well. This effect may undermine the YOLOv8n
object-detection performance.
Last, the clean-augmented combined dataset contains 3,000 clean images and 3,000 augmented images, with a
clean-to-augmented ratio of 1:1. This may have a negative effect on YOLOv8n performance, as a large portion of
augmented images may shift the domain to a “weather-specific” domain; the model overlearns features from the
augmented images while underrepresenting the clean images.
5.4 Summary
Overall, the results show that augmentation is most useful when it supplements clean training data instead of replacing
it. Training only on augmented images generally reduced performance on clear and rainy images, likely because the
augmented images create a domain gap between the augmented images and the clear images. In contrast, combined
training preserved clear-weather accuracy while adding exposure to adverse-weather appearances.
The biggest improvement on object detection robustness appears in the foggy split, where PBR plus GAN and
clean plus PBR plus GAN both outperformed the clean baseline. The rainy split did not show a clear improvement
over clean-only training; however, combined augmentation outperformed augmented-only training while preserving
performance close to clear-only training. These findings support the proposed framework as a useful dataset expansion
method, especially when real adverse-weather data are limited, unbalanced, or difficult to collect with controllable and
consistent labels.
6 Future Work
Future work should first separate the limitations of the augmentation framework from the effects of transfer learning.
In this study, YOLOv8n is initialized from pretrained weights, which may already contain useful features from mild
rain and fog. A stronger experiment would be training an object detection model from scratch, using the clear-baseline
dataset and augmented datasets. Models trained from random initialization should also be compared with the pretrained
detectors, so a conclusive result about the effect of transfer learning could be drawn.
The Cycle-GAN components should also be improved through larger and higher-resolution training. The current
Rain and Fog Cycle-GAN models were trained with images cropped at 384 × 384, which likely contributed to white
noise, over-desaturation, overexposure, and clustered fog artifacts. Future work could use higher-resolution training and
larger training domains for the training process. For fog, combining Cycle-GAN appearance transfer with a depth-aware
atmospheric scattering model (ASM), which is currently a mainstream solution to synthetic fog, may preserve the
realistic global style of generated fog while improving physical control over visibility and scene depth (Cordts et al.,
2016; Sakaridis et al., 2018).
Another direction is to improve the rainy target domain. Many BDD100K rainy images appear to show post-rainfall
scenes rather than active rainfall, so the Rain Cycle-GAN generator may only learn to generate subtle rain effects.
Future Rain Cycle-GAN training should consider using a separate active rainfall dataset or a combined active rainfall
plus rainy dataset for training. The generator would therefore learn a clearer target distribution, and the evaluation of
depth-aware rain rendering could be more reliable.
Evaluation should be expanded beyond the current BDD100K validation splits. The foggy validation set contains
only 13 images, so the observed improvement on foggy scenes is inconclusive. Future work should test on larger foggy
benchmarks. Cross-dataset evaluation would show whether the proposed augmentation improves robustness across
different cameras, locations, weather distributions, etc. Additional downstream tasks, such as image segmentation or
depth estimation, could also be tested to produce a study on the effect of data augmentation.
The current clean-plus-augmented training dataset for the YOLOv8n model contains 3,000 clean images and 3,000
augmented images. As discussed in subsection 5.3, this may lead to overrepresentation or underrepresentation of
specific features or domains. Future work should consider optimizing the clear-to-augmented ratio, so the object
detection model balances performance across adverse-weather conditions.

Finally, future work should study the effect of different severity levels on data augmentation. Due to the length of
this study, this work does not investigate or compare the effects of different rain severities on data augmentation. In fact,

7 Conclusion
This paper develops and evaluates a depth-aware data augmentation framework for simulating rain and fog distortions in
outdoor RGB driving images. The project was motivated by the difficulty of collecting real adverse-weather datasets at
scale, the imbalance across weather type and severity, and the scarcity of controlled conditions and labels for distortion
classification and detection training and systematic robustness testing. By applying controllable synthetic weather to
BDD100K images, the framework turns an existing clear-weather driving dataset into a controlled adverse-weather
dataset, making it usable for training object detection models.
The proposed rain pipeline combines two complementary ideas. The physics-based renderer provides direct control
over rain streak density, opacity, apparent depth, mist, and visibility, using Depth Anything V2 to prevent physically
inconsistent rain appearance across foreground and background objects. The Cycle-GAN model adds global rainy
appearance changes, including wet road surfaces, dimmer lighting, and desaturated skies. The hybrid PBR plus GAN
approach therefore combines global rainy-style appearance with controllable depth-aware rain synthesis. For fog,
a Cycle-GAN trained using the Cityscapes and Foggy Cityscapes datasets produces foggy BDD100K images with
reduced contrast, softened distant objects, and desaturated appearance.
The YOLOv8n evaluation shows mixed but useful results. On clear and rainy validation images, the clean baseline
remained strongest, reaching 0.1589 and 0.1689 mAP50–95, respectively. Augmented-only training generally reduced
performance, suggesting that synthetic weather can introduce a domain gap when it replaces clean data. However,
clean-plus-augmented training stayed close to the clean baseline. For example, clean plus depth-aware PBR reached
0.1583 on clear images and 0.1655 on rainy images, while clean plus PBR plus GAN reached 0.1580 and 0.1634. These
results suggest that weather augmentation is more reliable when it supplements clean data rather than replacing it.
The most promising improvement appeared on foggy images. The clean baseline achieved 0.1419 mAP50–95 on
the foggy split, while PBR plus GAN reached 0.2259 and clean plus PBR plus GAN reached 0.1987. This indicates
that adverse-weather augmentation may help detectors generalize to low-visibility scenes. However, because the foggy
validation split contains only 13 images, this result requires further validation on larger datasets.
Overall, the framework shows that depth-aware and data-driven augmentation can create useful controlled weather
variation, but its effectiveness depends on multiple factors, including training method, data size, and image resolution.
Future work should train Cycle-GAN models with larger and higher-resolution datasets, improve the rainy target domain
with active-rainfall images, combine fog translation with mainstream depth-aware atmospheric scattering models,
evaluate on larger foggy and cross-dataset benchmarks, compare pretrained and from-scratch object detection models,
and study different clean-to-augmented ratios and weather severity levels.
