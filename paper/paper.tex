\title{A Depth-Aware, Physics-Based Data Augmentation Framework for Simulating Rain and Fog
Distortions in Outdoor RGB Images}
\author{
Rongxuan Duan\\
Pioneer Research Institute\\
{\tt\small }
}
\date{}


\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Outdoor RGB driving datasets often underrepresent adverse-weather conditions such as rain and fog, making it difficult to train distortion-aware detection models and evaluate 
robustness systematically. This paper presents a depth-aware, physics-based augmentation framework for simulating rain and fog in outdoor driving images. 
Using BDD100K as the baseline dataset, the framework generates rainy images through image-plane rendering, depth-aware physics-based rendering using depth maps produced by Depth Anything V2, 
Cycle-GAN translation, and a hybrid Cycle-GAN plus physics-based pipeline. For fog simulation, a Cycle-GAN generator trained on Cityscapes and Foggy 
Cityscapes is applied to BDD100K while preserving object-detection labels. The augmented datasets are evaluated by training YOLOv8n detectors 
and testing them on clear, rainy, and foggy BDD100K validation splits. Results show that replacing clean training data with augmented data generally reduces 
clear-weather and rainy-weather detection performance, whereas combining clean and augmented data preserves performance close to the clean-data baseline. 
The strongest gains are observed on the foggy split, although this finding remains preliminary due to its limited foggy sample size. 
Overall, the framework shows that controlled adverse-weather augmentation is most effective as a supplement to clean training data and provides a practical 
foundation for future robustness studies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Images used in computer vision are often distorted. Distortion in computer vision refers to any signal that produces a visual deviation in an image/video 
from its expected appearance~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}. When the original image/video is unavailable, \textcite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer} also consider ``observed visual comfort or simply annoying effect
on the aesthetic visual appearance'' to be a distortion. These distortions can be
sorted into In-Capture Distortion, which occurs during the initial acquisition phase, including physical artifacts like blur, noise, uneven illumination, color shifts, haze,
rain, snow, and other visibility changes; and Post-Capture Distortion, which refers to image deformation or degradation that occurs after images are captured, such as transmission
impairments, enhancement artifacts, and adversarial distortion. 

Although these effects may
appear minor to human observers, they can change the statistical structure of an image and reduce
the reliability of machine learning models trained primarily on clean
data~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}. Distortions affect the
performance of high-level tasks, such as object classification, object detection, and scene
understanding~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}. High-level tasks are more sensitive to distortions than low-level tasks, such as 
edge detection, image enhancement, and image colorization, because models often learn specific, localized semantic features rather than global context.
Therefore, distortion detection and removal are possible preprocessing steps for high-level tasks to decrease the
impact on performance. \autoref{fig:nine-grid} demonstrates several common distortions in high-level tasks.

For machine learning, distortion is not only an image-quality issue but is also affected by the dataset and
training. Models usually learn from the distribution of inputs they are given, so a model
trained only on clean images may have poor generalizability when processing the same object
appearing under poor visibility, weather effects, or degraded contrast. Distortion-specific
datasets are important because they make it possible to evaluate model robustness by distortion type and severity, 
rather than reporting only a single clean-data accuracy score~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}.


Current datasets of natural distortions have important limitations. Real rain, haze, fog,
and snow images can be difficult to collect at scale, and the resulting data may be unbalanced
across weather type, severity, scene category, and annotation quality. Many benchmark datasets
also include distortions as broad corruptions rather than as physically meaningful,
scene-dependent effects. This limits how well they can support controlled robustness studies on deep learning models' performance under
different types of distortions or targeted training strategies, which introduce controlled distortions to train models for more stable performance when facing distortions.


Related work has explored both distortion recognition and natural-distortion simulation. Survey
work on distortion classification emphasizes that multiple and natural distortions remain
difficult to categorize and model
consistently~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}. In weather-focused vision,
rain simulation has been used to render controlled rain effects for robustness evaluation, while
haze and fog modeling often relies on atmospheric image-formation ideas such as scattering,
attenuation, and reduced scene contrast. Work on adverse-weather perception, including foggy
driving scenes, also shows that real weather can strongly affect downstream recognition and
detection performance.


My work uses a distortion augmentation framework, which adds controlled rain and fog
effects to an existing baseline dataset, BDD100K \cite{wolfeBDD100KLargescaleDiverse}. The framework is evaluated by comparing the
performance of a downstream object detection model, YOLOv8n~\cite{WhatYOLOv8InDepth,ExploreUltralyticsYOLOv82023}, on the original dataset and the
augmented dataset.


\section{Background}

A \textbf{Generative Adversarial Network} (GAN) is a deep learning model that consists of two networks that 
compete against each other: a generator G and a discriminator D. The generator G tries to generate 
realistic images from a target domain $Y$, while the discriminator D tries
to discriminate generated images from real images~\cite{goodfellowGenerativeAdversarialNets}. That is, the generator learns a mapping: $G: X \rightarrow Y$.
For example, in the context of my work, the generator G tries to create rainy- or foggy-looking images from clear images,
while the discriminator D tries to distinguish between real rainy or foggy images and generated images. The generator and
discriminator are trained in an adversarial manner, where the adversarial framework's objective is to minimize the generator
loss and maximize the discriminator loss. The loss function of GAN is given below, defined by Goodfellow et al.~\cite{goodfellowGenerativeAdversarialNets}.
\[
\min_G \max_D \quad \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z(z)}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))].
\]

Traditional GANs use paired training data, which consists of training examples $\{x_i, y_i\}_{i=1}^N$, where 
$x_i$ is the input and $y_i$ is the corresponding output~\cite{mondalSemisupervisedSemanticSegmentation}. GANs are useful for image-to-image translation tasks, such as photo 
reconstruction from edge maps, colorization, photo synthesis from label maps, etc.~\cite{isolaImagetoImageTranslationConditional2018}. 
However, in many cases, paired training data is not available. In the context of my work, it is difficult to collect paired
training data consisting of clear images and corresponding rainy or foggy images in the same places. Existing datasets, such as BDD100K, Cityscapes,
or ACDC, are all unpaired datasets\cite{wolfeBDD100KLargescaleDiverse,cordtsCityscapesDatasetSemantic2016,sakaridisACDCAdverseConditions2021}.
Figure~\ref{fig:paired-unpaired} illustrates the difference between paired and unpaired training data.

My work uses Cycle-GAN, which is a type of GAN that learns from unpaired training data. Cycle-GAN uses two generators and
two discriminators. A generator $G: X \rightarrow Y$ transforms images from domain $X$ to domain $Y$; a reverse generator
$F: Y \rightarrow X$ transforms images from domain $Y$ to domain $X$. The first discriminator $D_Y$ distinguishes between real images in domain $Y$ and 
generated images from generator $G$; the second discriminator $D_X$ distinguishes between real images in domain $X$ and generated images 
from generator $F$~\cite{zhuUnpairedImagetoImageTranslation2020}. The Cycle-GAN framework uses a cycle-consistency loss to ensure that the generated images can be transformed back to the original domain.
The cycle-consistency loss is given below, defined by Zhu et al.~\cite{zhuUnpairedImagetoImageTranslation2020}.
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cyc}}(G,F) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\mathrm{data}}(x)} \left[\|F(G(x)) - x\|_1\right] + \mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_{\mathrm{data}}(y)} \left[\|G(F(y)) - y\|_1\right].
\]

The Cycle-GAN tries to minimize the adversarial loss, while maximizing the discriminator loss and minimizing
the cycle-consistency loss. If an image is transformed from domain $X$ to domain $Y$ by G and then is transformed back to domain $X$ by F, 
then the resulting image should be similar to the original image. This is the key idea of Cycle-GAN -- it preserves features from the original image despite using unpaired training. This cycle-consistency allows the model not only to produce an image that looks like it belongs to the target domain,
but also to preserve the content, or the structure of the original image\cite{zhuUnpairedImagetoImageTranslation2020}.

In weather augmentation, Cycle-GAN is useful because it generates realistic rainy or foggy images from clear images while it preserves the scene structures, and it does not require paired training data. 
However, a limitation of Cycle-GAN, as well as other GANs, is that it is not controllable. It is impossible to control how much wetness, rain, or fog is generated,
and it is impossible to control the depth-dependent effects of rain and fog\cite{zhuUnpairedImagetoImageTranslation2020,tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021}. Therefore, this work uses Cycle-GAN to generate rainy and foggy images, but it also uses
a physics-based, depth-aware renderer to generate rain with controllable appearance.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Distortion Classification and Evaluation}

Traditional distortion classification and evaluation models utilize blur indices, noise estimators,
natural scene statistics, image quality metrics for distortion severity evaluation, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and K-Nearest
Neighbors (KNN) for distortion classification\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}. Recent distortion classification and
detection models use Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and temporal models. \textcite{ahnImageDistortionDetection2017} train a Y-shaped CNN for joint distortion classification and localization. Other methods
for distortion classification include feature pooling, relating metrics with specific distortions,
and Natural Scene Statistics (NSS)~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}. These methods support learned distortion features, yet most
datasets focus on controlled distortions rather than natural
weather.


The quality of visual content in images and distortion severity are important factors that
affect performance in high-level tasks, including object classification, object detection, and
scene understanding. Using distortion classification and detection models could help
researchers fine-tune models that are more resistant to distortions. However, my work helps
fine-tune models by presenting a data augmentation framework that increases models'
resilience to rain and fog distortions during training and does not require a distortion
detection and removal process.


\subsection[Current Limitations of Distortion Datasets]{%
Current Limitations of Distortion\\
Datasets}


Current distortion datasets give labels and severity levels, but many examples come from
synthetic, single-type, quality-assessment settings. LIVE, TID2008, TID2013, CSIQ, Waterloo,
KADID-10K, LIVEMD, and MDID help keep a controlled evaluation. However, they do not contain
natural weather with precise physical labels. Rain, haze, fog, and snow depend on lighting, exposure, particle
density, scene depth, and occlusion. There is a lack of labeled, controlled multi-distortion
datasets. Consequently, classification and localization of multiple distortions remain
challenging due to a lack of labeled, controlled
datasets~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer,
tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021}. My work adds controlled, artificial distortions, which
addresses the lack of labeled, controlled datasets, especially for outdoor datasets.


\subsection{Traditional Rain Rendering}

Garg and Nayar model rain streak photometry and spatio-temporal behavior. Their work links rain
visibility to drop size, velocity, exposure time, scene radiance, camera settings, and drop
shape oscillation. This result matters for rendering because a transparent 2D texture ignores
camera and scene factors. Traditional rain rendering lacks the effect of moisture on the scene,
and it often appears to be unrealistic. For instance, traditional rain rendering lacks wet
surfaces such as water on the ground, and it creates raindrops that are supposed to be hidden
behind buildings~\cite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021}.
However, this framework supported rain detection, removal, rendering, and camera-based rain
measurement~\cite{gargVisionRain2007}.

\subsection{Modern Rain Rendering}

Modern rain rendering turns rain models into dataset augmentation for robustness tests. Halder,
Lalonde, and de Charette combine a particle simulator, outdoor illumination estimation, and
rain photometry to render rain and fog over clear driving images. The pipeline preserves labels
from KITTI and Cityscapes while changing weather appearance. Detection and segmentation results
then measure model sensitivity under controlled
rain~\cite{halderPhysicsBasedRenderingImproving2019}.


\textcite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021} compare three rendering routes: physics-based,
data-driven, and hybrid. They classify raindrops into rain particles and fog-like rain.
Fog-like rain is a single pixel that contains multiple rain particles; it is located far away from
the camera and appears as fog. The physics-based route combines fog-like rain, rain particles,
and photometric modeling. The renderer estimates outdoor illumination because a raindrop
receives light from a wider angular range (approximately 160 degrees) than the camera's view (70
to 100 degrees). This design improves physical control over a flat overlay. The same design also
depends on sky visibility, environmental lighting or photometry symmetry, and compute
cost~\cite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021,hold-geoffroyDeepOutdoorIllumination2018,
zhangAllWeatherDeepOutdoor2019}.


The data-driven route uses a GAN-style image translation model. A GAN trains two networks: a
generator synthesizes target-domain images, and a discriminator judges whether each image looks
real or generated. Adversarial training on the generator outputs rainy-looking images. The
output from the generator contains features such as dark sky, rainy environment, wet surfaces,
and dim illumination. However, GAN is less controllable, which risks rain density, depth
ordering, and scene consistency. GAN's focus on global appearance also creates unrealistic
features, such as fake water
reflections~\cite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021,
isolaImagetoImageTranslationConditional2018,huangMultimodalUnsupervisedImagetoImage2018}.


\textcite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021} render rain over KITTI, Cityscapes, and nuScenes. They evaluate object
detection, semantic segmentation, and depth estimation under increasing degradation. The paper
reports degraded performance as rain intensity increases and more robust performance after
fine-tuning using rain-augmented datasets. The paper reports that outputs from GAN only are
user-rated as the most realistic images; outputs from PBR plus GAN perform best for data
augmentation~\cite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021}.


My work is inspired by the framework from Tremblay et al. My work uses the traditional rain
rendering framework with depth estimation to build controlled,
depth-dependent rain streaks; my work also involves using a Cycle-GAN model that adds rainy
and foggy visual effects.


\subsection{Fog Rendering}

Fog rendering often follows the atmospheric image formation model. Scene radiance attenuates
with distance, while airlight grows along the camera's ray. \textcite{sakaridisSemanticFoggyScene2018} apply
this model to clear Cityscapes images and produce Foggy Cityscapes. Using depth makes fog stronger
in far regions rather than applying it uniformly across the image. Training with synthetic fog improves
semantic foggy scene understanding, but results depend on depth quality, density choices, and
the synthetic-to-real gap~\cite{sakaridisSemanticFoggyScene2018}.

Clear2Fog extends this direction with monocular depth estimation and atmospheric light
estimation. Monocular depth estimation does not require 3D constructions such as LiDAR; rather,
this paper uses Apple Depth Pro to produce an estimated depth map. The pipeline simulates fog
on clear-weather driving datasets and tries to preserve camera-LiDAR consistency. Without
relying on LiDAR, Clear2Fog remains stable under extreme conditions like fog, snow or haze.
\textcite{bijelicSeeingFogSeeing2020} focus on multimodal perception rather than rendering.
The dataset and fusion model show different failure patterns across camera, LiDAR, radar, and
gated near-infrared
sensors~\cite{mohamedDataEfficiencyStudy2026,bijelicSeeingFogSeeing2020}.


\subsection{Depth Estimation and Application in Distortion Rendering}

Depth provides the geometry needed for natural distortion placement. In haze and fog, depth
sets attenuation and airlight strength. In rain, depth helps determine the severity,
intensity and placement of rain streaks. A renderer without geometry often places
plausible-looking weather in physically inconsistent
locations~\cite{sakaridisSemanticFoggyScene2018,tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021}.


Depth estimation models estimate distance from an image or complete sparse sensor-depth data. The
output supplies an approximate geometry map when a dataset lacks dense ground truth. Jaritz et
al. study sparse and dense depth completion for driving scenes. Depth Anything V2 offers a
modern monocular option trained with synthetic images and pseudo-labeled real images. This
makes Depth Anything V2 useful for RGB-only distortion
rendering~\cite{jaritzSparseDenseData2018,yangDepthAnythingV22024}.


My work uses the depth map output from Depth Anything V2 as the guiding parameter for
the transparency and appearance of rain streaks and the translucency and intensity of fog. The
depth-map output from Depth Anything V2 is stable and reliable, and it is a strong model for
completing my computer vision tasks.


\subsection{Datasets With Distortions}

General distortion and corruption datasets support baselines, but natural weather rendering
requires more geometry and scene context. The distortion survey lists LIVE, TID2008, TID2013,
CSIQ, Waterloo, KADID-10K, LIVEMD, and MDID as major image-quality or multi-distortion
resources. ImageNet-C standardizes common corruption robustness for image classifiers. These
resources help robustness framing. They do not model depth-aware rain, haze, or fog~\cite{muhammadaliqureshiDistortionClassificationComputer}.


Weather-specific datasets match the project more closely. ACDC includes fog, nighttime, rain,
and snow with dense driving-scene annotations and normal-condition correspondences for many
scenes. Foggy Cityscapes and rain-augmented KITTI, Cityscapes, and nuScenes preserve existing
labels while adding synthetic weather. The limitation of these datasets is the lack of
controlled severity or intensity, depth-aware labels, and controlled natural
distortions~\cite{sakaridisACDCAdverseConditions2021,sakaridisSemanticFoggyScene2018,
tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021}.


\subsection{BDD100K}

BDD100K serves as a baseline dataset for my work, because the data are large, diverse, and
driving-focused. The dataset contains 100,000 driving videos and supports detection,
segmentation, lane marking, drivable area, and related driving labels. \autoref{fig:bdd100k} shows three images with various weather labels from the BDD100K dataset. The BDD100K dataset also includes
geographic, environmental, and weather variation. The limitations are missing paired
clean/adverse views and controlled severity labels. A depth-aware rain and fog renderer would
turn BDD100K into a controlled robustness testbed while keeping real driving diversity~\cite{yuBDD100KDiverseDriving2018}.

\section{Methodology}

Data augmentation is a strategy for improving computer vision models' performance by artificially 
increasing the diversity of training images through transformations such as cropping, 
rotating, flipping and adding noise. These methods are especially useful when access to 
labeled image data is limited, because they expose a model to variations it may encounter
in real-world visual tasks. In computer vision, data augmentation helps image classifiers
learn features that are less dependent on a single viewpoint, orientation, or image style, 
reducing overfitting and improving generalization \cite{perezEffectivenessDataAugmentation2017}. 

Data augmentation is the primary method to increase a dataset's size and variety. 
In the context of this paper, it refers to adding rain and fog to images. As discussed 
in previous sections, collecting data with various natural distortions for an outdoor 
dataset is challenging. Therefore, using data augmentation to render natural distortions 
is expected to improve performance of deep learning models. My work focuses on rain and fog 
distortions in outdoor RGB images.

\subsection{Rain Rendering}

Rain appears as dynamic-looking particles and brings a rainy effect to the image. Inspired by 
Tremblay et al.~\cite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021}, my physics-based rain rendering method that simulates raindrops builds on depth estimation and uses a Cycle-GAN model that creates rainy effects. 

\subsubsection{Simple Rain Rendering}

Simple rain rendering is used as a baseline physics-based augmentation method. This method does not use a depth map or estimate the 3D position of objects in the scene. 
Instead, it adds rain directly in the 2D image plane. The purpose of this method is to create a controllable comparison point for evaluating whether depth information improves the realism and usefulness of rain augmentation.

Given an input RGB/BGR image $I$ with height $H$ and width $W$, the simple rain renderer first defines an image area scale $A=HW/1000$ and a minimum image dimension $m=\min(H,W)$. A severity value $s$ controls the strength of the rain. 
In the simple rain rendering setting, three severities (``light'', ``medium'', ``heavy'') refer to three fixed severity values, each determining the number of rain streaks, the streak length, the streak width, opacity, etc.
Higher severity values produce denser rain, longer motion trails, and a darker rainy appearance.

The renderer creates rain using three 2D streak layers. These layers represent different visual scales of rain: 
a layer of longer and thicker streaks, a layer of medium streaks, and a layer of shorter fine streaks. 
Each layer $l$ has a streak count $N_l$, streak length $L_l$, streak width $W_l$, base opacity $\alpha_l$, brightness coefficient $\epsilon_l$, and shadow suppression coefficient $\eta_l$. 
For example, the longest-streak layer uses:

\begin{equation}
N_1=A(0.45+1.55s) \quad L_1=m(0.025+0.042s) \quad W_1=1.15+0.75s \quad \alpha_1=0.050+0.095s
\label{eq:simple-layer-params}
\end{equation}

The medium and fine layers follow the same structure, but use shorter and thinner streaks 
with larger particle counts. This layered design prevents the rain pattern from looking like a 
single repeated texture, serving as a simplified solution to simulate the depth-related appearance of rain.

Individual rain streaks are generated using an anisotropic motion kernel, which is a kernel that blurs
differently in different directions, rather than hard lines. The streaks start with a fixed streak angle of 105 degrees, with slight random variations within streaks and images. 
For a streak angle $\theta$, each kernel pixel is projected into coordinates along and across the
rain direction, inspired by \textcite{bakosPrototypeInteractiveDynamic}:
\begin{equation}
u=x\cos\theta + y\sin\theta \quad v = -x\,\sin\theta + y\cos\theta
\label{eq:rain-kernel-coordinates}
\end{equation}

where $u$ is distance along the rain streak and $v$ is distance across the rain streak.

The streak intensity (how strong each pixel of a rain streak is inside the filter) is tapered along its length
and Gaussian blurred along its width:
\begin{equation}
K(u,v)=\left[\max\left(0,1-\frac{|u|}{L/2}\right)\right]^p \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{v}{\sigma_w}\right)^2\right)
\label{eq:anisotropic-motion-kernel}
\end{equation}
where $p=1.45$ controls tapering and:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_w = \max(0.35, 0.55W_l)
\label{eq:rain-kernel-width}
\end{equation}

The kernel is normalized, so its peak value is one. Applying this kernel to a point-like rain particle creates
motion-blurred rain streaks that resemble real rain's photometry rather than opaque white streaks.

The brightness of each streak is adjusted according to the local image brightness. The renderer converts the image to linear light and computes a local luminance value $Y$ for each sampled point:
\begin{equation}
Y=0.0722B+0.7152G+0.2126R
\label{eq:linear-luminance}
\end{equation}

Rain is more visible over darker regions and less visible over bright regions, so the contrast factor is defined as:
\begin{equation}
C=0.26+0.72(1-Y)^{0.78}
\label{eq:simple-contrast}
\end{equation}

The pixel intensity change for a sampled streak is then:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_i=\epsilon_l C-\eta_l Y^{1.20}+\mathcal{N}(0,2.2+2.8s)
\label{eq:simple-delta}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_l$ controls highlight strength and $\eta_l$ suppresses overly bright streaks on bright backgrounds. The opacity of each streak is sampled from a log-normal distribution:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_i=\operatorname{clip}(\alpha_l \cdot LogNormal(0,0.20),0.0015,0.180)
\label{eq:simple-opacity}
\end{equation}
This random variation avoids perfectly uniform rain streaks across the image.

The simple renderer accumulates all streaks into an optical thickness map $\tau(x,y)$ and a brightness-change map $\tau\Delta(x,y)$. The accumulated brightness change is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\tau\Delta(x,y)=\sum_i \tau_i(x,y)\Delta_i
\label{eq:rain-delta-accumulation}
\end{equation}
The final rain alpha is then computed as:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{rain}=\operatorname{clip}(1-e^{-\tau(2.10+1.20s)},0,0.42)
\label{eq:simple-rain-alpha}
\end{equation}
The final rain color change $\Delta_{rain}$ is the opacity-weighted average of the accumulated streak brightness changes:
\[
\Delta_{rain}(x,y)=\frac{\tau\Delta(x,y)}{\tau(x,y)+\epsilon}
\]

The rain color change $\Delta_{rain}$ is summed with the original image $I$ to get $I_{rain}$, the original image with simple rain streaks added.

Before compositing the streaks, the renderer slightly darkens the whole image to imitate reduced illumination during rain; the darkened image is denoted as $I_{dark}$. 
The final image is formed by blending the darkened image with the rain-adjusted image according to $\alpha_{rain}$:

\begin{equation}
I_{out} = I_{dark}(1-\alpha_{rain}) + I_{rain}\alpha_{rain}
\end{equation}

Overall, simple rain rendering is fast, controllable, and does not require a depth-estimation model. 
The simple rain rendering method is applied to the BDD100K dataset, generating 3000 simple-rain augmented images and saving the object detection labels in YOLOv8 format.
\autoref{fig:simple-rain} contains three examples from the simple rain augmented BDD100K dataset.
Its main limitation is that it does not decide whether a rain streak should appear in front of or behind nearby objects. 
As a result, streaks may be placed uniformly across cars, buildings, road signs, and background regions even when this placement is not physically accurate. This limitation motivates the depth-aware physics-based renderer described in the next subsection.

\subsubsection{Depth-Aware Physics-Based Rain Rendering}
To generate synthetic raindrop appearance with scene geometry, the depth-aware physics-based rain rendering (PBR) method combines
image-space rain streak synthesis, atmospheric scattering, and linear-light compositing, based on the
monocular depth map produced by Depth Anything V2~\cite{yangDepthAnythingV22024}. The objective of this
renderer is to produce controllable raindrops while remaining consistent with scene geometry and depth.

Given an input RGB/BGR image $I$, Depth Anything V2 outputs an estimated depth map $D$ from the input $I$. The raw depth values are normalized into a relative distance map:
\[
D_n = \frac{\operatorname{clip}(D, p_1, p_{99})-\min(D)}{\max(D)-\min(D) + \epsilon}
\]
where $p_1$ and $p_{99}$ are the 1st and 99th percentiles of the depth values used to exclude extreme values, and $\epsilon$ is 1e-6 for avoiding division by zero. 
The normalized scene distance is then defined as $d(x,y) = 1 - D_{n}(x,y)$, where $d(x,y)=0$ represents foreground regions close to the camera and
$d(x,y)=1$ represents distant scene regions.

The renderer models rain as layered rain streaks. Four rain-depth layers are used: near, mid, far, and background.
Each layer $l$ has a fixed normalized depth $z_l$, a streak count $N_l$, streak length $L_l$, streak width $W_l$, base opacity $\alpha_l$, 
defocus blur, and angular jitter. For an image of height H, width W, minimum dimension $m = \min(H, W)$, 
area scale $A=HW/1000$, and rain severity $s$, the renderer follows the same parameter structure as the simple renderer in \autoref{eq:simple-layer-params}. 
For the three severities (``light'', ``medium'', ``heavy''), each severity corresponds to a fixed severity value $s$, and the formulae for other parameters are constructed through experimentation. 
The layer parameters are defined as functions of severity. For example, 
the near layer uses:
\[
N_{near}=A(0.025+0.13s) \quad L_{near}=m(0.025+0.080s) \quad W_{near}=1.75+2.20s \quad \alpha_{near}=0.020+0.055s
\]

The mid, far, and background layers follow the same structure, with progressively smaller streak widths and lengths but larger particle counts.
This models the physical appearance of raindrops, which appear to be smaller and more numerous as they are positioned further from an observer. 
Nearby raindrops appear longer, thicker and more defocused, while distant raindrops appear denser and smaller.

Depth is used to determine whether rain in a given layer should be visible at each pixel. A rain layer is only visible when it lies in front of the visible
scene surface. Raindrops from a given layer do not suddenly appear or disappear; rather, they fade in or out when their normalized depth $D_n(x,y)$ is at a depth-edge, where it is 
close to a layer's fixed normalized depth $z_l$. A visibility function tells how visible a rain streak at the coordinate $(x,y)$ is, and it is computed using a smooth transition function:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
V_l(x,y)=smoothstep(-\delta_l, \delta_l, d(x,y)-z_l)\\
V_l: [0,1]\rightarrow[0,1]
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:depth-layer-visibility}
\end{equation}

where $\delta_l$ is the layer softness parameter that controls how rapidly the raindrops fade in or out. A smaller $\delta_l$ means 
a sharper transition, while a larger $\delta_l$ means a smoother transition. The near layer has the smallest $\delta_l$, while the background layer has the largest
$\delta_l$. The smoothstep function is:

\[
\begin{aligned}
smoothstep(a, b, x) = t^2(3-2t) \\
t = \operatorname{clip}(\frac{x-a}{b-a+\epsilon}, 0, 1)
\end{aligned}
\]

In the smoothstep function, $t$ is clipped to a value between 0 and 1. Therefore, the domain of visibility $V_l$ is $[0, 1]$, and its range is $[0, 1]$.
The defined visibility function using the smoothstep function is a special cubic function with three inputs, and it gradually decreases in its domain $[0, 1]$. Therefore, a streak that is closer has
a larger visibility value, while a streak that is more distant has a smaller visibility value.

This prevents distant rain from being visible in front of foreground objects. The smoothstep function and visibility
function prevent discontinuities in the rain appearance at a depth-edge. It also prevents halo artifacts around object boundaries.

Individual rain streaks are then generated using the same anisotropic motion kernel defined in \autoref{eq:rain-kernel-coordinates}, \autoref{eq:anisotropic-motion-kernel}, and \autoref{eq:rain-kernel-width}. The difference is that each depth-aware layer is multiplied by the visibility function in \autoref{eq:depth-layer-visibility}, so the same motion-streak model is clipped against the estimated scene geometry rather than being placed uniformly across the image.

For each sampled rain streak, the opacity follows the same log-normal sampling idea as \autoref{eq:simple-opacity}. However, the depth-aware renderer uses 
a slightly wider log-normal distribution and clips the result to the range $(0.001,0.18)$:
\[
\alpha_i=\operatorname{clip}(\alpha_l \cdot LogNormal(0,0.22),0.001,0.180)
\]
This allows the opacity to change gradually, creating a smooth degradation in opacity as the streaks get more distant. 
The brightness level $Y$ is computed from the same linear-light luminance equation in \autoref{eq:linear-luminance}. 
The rain streak contrast factor also follows the brightness-dependent design in \autoref{eq:simple-contrast}, but uses the depth-aware constants:
\[
C=0.34+1.05(1-Y)^{0.72}
\]
The pixel intensity delta follows the same form as \autoref{eq:simple-delta}, but it uses depth-aware constants and is clipped to prevent extreme values:
\[
\Delta_i=\operatorname{clip}(\epsilon_l C-\eta_lY^{1.15}+\mathcal{N}(0,4+5\alpha_l), -16, 135)
\]

The renderer then accumulates rain as an optical thickness map in the same way as \autoref{eq:rain-delta-accumulation}. Each raindrop's pixel intensity $\Delta_i$, convolved with the motion kernel, is added to the map. The optical thickness map is denoted as $\tau(x,y)$, the same as in simple rain rendering. 
The final rain alpha follows the same optical-thickness form as \autoref{eq:simple-rain-alpha}, but uses depth-aware clipping:
\[
\alpha_{rain} = \operatorname{clip}(1-e^{-\operatorname{clip}(\tau,0,3.5)},0,0.62)
\]

The final rain color delta map $\Delta_{rain}$ is the opacity-weighted average and clipped within the plausible range $(-18, 145)$:
\[
\Delta_{rain}(x,y)=\operatorname{clip}\left(\frac{\tau\Delta(x,y)}{\tau(x,y)+\epsilon}, -18, 145\right)
\]
This restricts the values of rain color differences to a reasonable range.

A light mist layer is applied before compositing the streaks, based on the depth map. The mist intensity depends on the layer, where a more distant layer has a higher intensity.
A smoothstep function is applied, preventing abrupt changes in mist appearance between layers. 
The mist map is denoted as $I_{mist}$, and it is then added to the slightly tinted 
rain delta map $\Delta_{rain}$ to produce a weather augmentation mask, $I_{target}$.

The output image is then:
\[
I_{out} = I_{mist}(1-\alpha_{rain})+I_{target} \alpha_{rain}
\]

Overall, the renderer combines depth estimation with physics-based optical accumulation. \autoref{fig:depth-aware-rain} includes three input-output pairs, juxtaposing the original and the output images for comparison. 
The depth-based layer visibility prevents rain from incorrectly appearing over foreground objects.
The optical thickness map $\tau$ blends the opacities of rain streaks that are co-linear in a line from the observer, 
rather than overlaying rain streaks directly onto the input image, causing a locally unrealistic exposure. The brightness-aware streaks ensure that brightness is adjustable 
depending on the background and depth.

\subsubsection{Rain Cycle-GAN}
\label{sec:section-rain-gan}

While the physics-based renderer simulates raindrops and fine mist, it lacks rainy characteristics 
such as wetness, reflections, sky, and lighting. Scenes generated from the physics-based renderer 
may have realistic raindrops and mist, but they lack an overall rainy look. These characteristics 
are hard to generate through a controlled method, whereas Cycle-GAN can handle image-to-image translation 
tasks.

The dataset used for training is BDD100K. It contains seven labels: “clear”, “overcast”, “undefined”, 
“snowy”, “rainy”, “partly cloudy” and “foggy”. The corresponding numbers of images in the training split are 
37344, 8700, 8119, 5549, 5070, 5881, 130, respectively; the corresponding numbers of images in the validation 
split are 5346, 1239, 1157, 769, 738, 738, 13, respectively. Labels “clear” and “rainy” are used for 
training, which refer to clear weather and rainy weather, respectively. 

For unpaired Cycle-GAN training, the training process loads 3000 clear images and 3000 rainy
images. Training images are loaded at $512\times512$ and then randomly cropped at $384\times384$. The model 
is optimized for 10 epochs and 10 decaying epochs, a learning rate of 0.0001 and a batch size of 1.

\subsubsection{Framework Combining PBR and Rain Cycle-GAN}

The final rain augmentation framework combines the Rain Cycle-GAN model and the depth-aware physics-based renderer into a hybrid pipeline.
The purpose is to use the strengths of both methods. Rain Cycle-GAN generates the overall rainy appearance, while the physics-based rain renderer adds controllable
rainfall, including rain streaks, fine mist, and depth-aware visibility.

The pipeline starts with a clear-weather image from BDD100K. First, the image is passed through the trained Rain Cycle-GAN generator to produce a rainy-looking output of the same scene.
The output preserves structures such as road layout, vehicles, buildings, and other structures; it shifts the image toward the rainy domain. 
Depth Anything V2 also estimates a monocular depth map from the input image, which is used by the PBR renderer.

After the Cycle-GAN translation, the PBR renderer composites rain onto the GAN-generated image. The severity parameter controls the
number and appearance of rain streaks. Near rain layers contain larger and more visible streaks, while far layers contain smaller and denser streaks.
The depth map prevents inconsistent results, such as distant rain appearing in front of nearby cars or buildings.

The order is important. If the physics-based rain is added before Cycle-GAN, the generator may weaken or distort the synthetic rain streaks.
By applying PBR after Cycle-GAN, the result may consistently keep the global rainy style while adding controllable rain.

Compared with Cycle-GAN alone, the hybrid framework provides direct control over rain intensity and depth placement. Compared with PBR alone, 
it produces a more complete rainy scene because it includes wetness and lighting changes. Therefore, the combined PBR and Rain Cycle-GAN framework
resembles real rainy images better than PBR or GAN alone, and it can produce controlled rainy training data with a smaller visual gap between synthetic rain and real rainy scenes.

In addition, \autoref{fig:comparison-rain} compares the clear image, the depth map produced by Depth Anything V2, and all four rain augmentation approaches: simple rain, PBR, Rain Cycle-GAN, and PBR plus GAN. 
The images involving PBR appear to be more realistic than the image using simple rain; the PBR plus GAN approach appears to be the most realistic and coherent over the entire scene visually.

\subsection{Fog Augmentation}
Foggy images usually contain bright skies, reduced contrast, desaturated colors, softened distant objects, and lower visibility across the scene. Although fog can be simulated 
with physics-based atmospheric scattering models (ASM), realistic fog depends on depth, illumination, atmospheric light, and camera exposure~\cite{cordtsCityscapesDatasetSemantic2016}. Simple brightness or color transformations are therefore 
not enough to produce convincing foggy driving scenes. In this work, I use Cycle-GAN for fog augmentation because it learns the overall visual style of fog from data while approximately 
preserving the road layout, vehicles, buildings, and other scene structures from the input image.

BDD100K contains very few foggy images: there are only 130 foggy images in the training split and 13 in the validation split. Since this is too small for stable, robust Cycle-GAN training,
I trained the Fog Cycle-GAN model using the Cityscapes clear images as domain $X$ and Foggy Cityscapes as domain $Y$~\cite{cordtsCityscapesDatasetSemantic2016,sakaridisSemanticFoggyScene2018}. 
The Foggy Cityscapes dataset is synthetically generated from the clean Cityscapes images for computer vision training, using the Atmospheric Scattering Model (ASM), which blends clear-weather 
images with a uniform atmospheric light based on scene depth and a fog density coefficient~\cite{cordtsCityscapesDatasetSemantic2016}. 
It supports more consistent and stable training for my Fog Cycle-GAN model because it contains urban driving images similar to the foggy BDD100K subset.

The Fog Cycle-GAN follows the same unpaired image-to-image translation framework as the Rain Cycle-GAN~\cite{zhuUnpairedImagetoImageTranslation2020}. 
Domain $X$ contains clear Cityscapes images, and domain $Y$ contains Foggy Cityscapes images. A fog density of 0.02 refers to how quickly visibility decreases over distance. 
This density corresponds to a visibility range of around 150 meters, and it is the densest fog density in the Foggy Cityscapes dataset~\cite{cordtsCityscapesDatasetSemantic2016}. It is chosen to create a more distinct difference between 
domain $X$ (the clear images) and domain $Y$ (the foggy images). The training dataset contains 3000 clear images 
and 3000 foggy images, with 400 clear and 400 foggy images used for testing. Images are loaded at width 512 and randomly cropped to 384 
during training. The model is trained using a batch size of 1, a learning rate of 0.0001, an identity loss weight of 1.0, no horizontal flipping, 10 epochs 
with constant learning rate, and 10 decaying epochs.

After training, the clear-to-fog generator is applied to the same 3000 clear images from the BDD100K dataset used in rain augmentation, generating 3000 clear-foggy image pairs.
The generated outputs are resized back to the original BDD100K image dimensions and saved as fog-augmented training images. Since Cycle-GAN changes the visual appearance but preserves 
the main scene layout, the original BDD100K object-detection labels are reused and exported in YOLOv8 format. This produces a fog-augmented BDD100K training set with 3000 images and 
corresponding bounding-box labels.

The Fog Cycle-GAN outputs preserve input structures, and they contain most foggy features, such as washed-out skies, reduced contrast, desaturated colors, softened distant objects, and lower visibility, as shown in \autoref{fig:fog-cycle-gan}.

\section{Analysis}

\textbf{YOLOv8} is a single-stage object detection model used to locate and classify objects in an image. It takes in an input image and predicts
object locations and class labels in one forward pass. For driving images, the model outputs bounding boxes around traffic-relevant objects
such as cars, trucks, buses, motorcycles, pedestrians, traffic lights and signs. Each predicted detection includes a bounding box, a confidence score,
and a class label. YOLO models serve as the baseline object detection models, and they are designed for efficient detection~\cite{ExploreUltralyticsYOLOv82023,WhatYOLOv8InDepth}. 

This project uses the YOLOv8n model, which is a lightweight nano version of YOLOv8, as a downstream detection model to be trained on the rain and fog augmented datasets. 
Specifically, YOLOv8n is trained and evaluated on clear, rainy, foggy, and augmented BDD100K image splits. Its performance on
object detection tasks is then used for comparison. \autoref{fig:yolo-examples} illustrates the predicted bounding boxes by the YOLOv8n model, randomly picked from the validation process.

\subsection{Metrics}

The performance of YOLOv8n is evaluated collectively on mAP50--95 as the main metric, with three other metrics for reference: precision, recall and mAP50~\cite{ExploreUltralyticsYOLOv82023,WhatYOLOv8InDepth}. 
When the predicted class correctly matches the ground-truth class and the Intersection over Union (IoU) between the two boxes is equal or above a required threshold, the predicted bounding box is considered as ``matched'' to a ground-truth bounding box~\cite{szeliski2011computer}. 
IoU is computed as:
\[
\text{IoU}=\frac{\text{area}(B_p \cap B_g)}{\text{area}(B_p \cup B_g)}
\]
where $B_p$ is the predicted box and $B_g$ is the ground-truth box.

For a specific threshold, a true positive (TP) is when the predicted class is correct, the IoU reaches that specific threshold, and the prediction is the highest-confidence 
unmatched prediction for that ground-truth object. A false positive (FP) is when the predicted class is wrong, the IoU is below the threshold being evaluated, or there is a duplicate of a ground-truth 
object that has already been matched by a higher-confidence prediction. A false negative (FN) is when no valid prediction is matched at the threshold being evaluated.
Precision is computed as $\frac{TP}{TP+FP}$, and recall is computed as $\frac{TP}{TP+FN}$~\cite{manning2008introduction}. These are reported by the Ultralytics YOLOv8 validation routine at the confidence operating point that maximizes the combined precision and recall, using an IoU threshold of 0.50 to determine true-positive matches~\cite{ExploreUltralyticsYOLOv82023,WhatYOLOv8InDepth}.

Average Precision (AP) is computed from the precision-recall curve for each object class by sorting predictions by confidence score and measuring how precision and recall change as more predictions are accepted~\cite{wangCWDETREfficientDetection2026}. 
Mean Average Precision (mAP) computes the averaged AP across all evaluated object classes. For mAP50, the required overlap threshold is $\text{IoU} \geq 0.50$. For mAP50--95, AP is computed repeatedly at IoU thresholds $0.50, 0.55, 0.60, \ldots, 0.95$, and the results are averaged. 
Therefore, mAP50 measures detection performance using a moderate localization requirement, while mAP50--95 is stricter as higher IoU thresholds require the predicted bounding boxes to be more accurate~\cite{ExploreUltralyticsYOLOv82023,WhatYOLOv8InDepth}. 
These metrics allow the experiment to compare YOLOv8n detection performance from varied points of view, following mAP-based object detection evaluation used in established works such as \textcite{tremblayRainRenderingEvaluating2021} and \textcite{mohamedDataEfficiencyStudy2026}.

\subsection{Results}

To evaluate whether the proposed augmentation framework improves object detection robustness, YOLOv8n was trained with different versions of the BDD100K training data and tested on clear, 
rainy, and foggy validation splits. The training images are resized from $1280\times720$ to $640\times640$. The training datasets include a clean baseline dataset containing 3,000 images, augmented-image-only datasets obtained by applying simple rain, PBR, Rain Cycle-GAN, PBR plus GAN, and fog augmentation
on the same 3,000 images in the clean baseline dataset, and datasets containing 3,000 augmented images combined with 3,000 clear images.
The main metric is mAP50--95, with precision, recall, and mAP50 reported for additional context. The clear validation split contains 5,346 images, 
the rainy split contains 738 images, and the foggy split contains 13 images. Note that the foggy validation split contains very few images, which requires extra caution when drawing conclusions.

\autoref{tab:clear-weather-results} summarizes the performances of YOLOv8n models on 5,346 clear BDD100K images, trained with combinations of clean training dataset and the augmented datasets. 
The model trained on clean baseline BDD100K images achieved the highest mAP50--95 score of 0.1589. Models trained only on augmented rain or fog data performed worse, showing that 
replacing clean images with synthetic weather images can reduce performance on normal inputs. However, the combined-training models stayed close to the clean baseline. 
Clean plus depth-aware PBR reached 0.1583 mAP50--95, and clean plus PBR plus GAN reached 0.1580, both within 0.001 of the clean-only model.

\subsection{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:section-limitations}

YOLOv8n models trained with the augmented-training methods are expected to outperform the model trained with the baseline method; 
however, the clear-baseline training method performs best in clear-image and rainy-image evaluations. Several reasons could potentially contribute to this failure.

First, the training process uses transfer learning. YOLOv8n is initialized from pretrained weights that were already learned from large-scale natural image datasets. 
These large-scale natural image datasets likely contain adverse-weather images, so the clean baseline model may already generalize reasonably well to mild rainy and foggy validation images without needing synthetic weather augmentation.
When the model is fine-tuned on augmented images, especially synthetic images that may contain artifacts or domain gaps, the training process may 
decrease the model's ability to generalize well across all features and maintain a stable performance under adverse conditions.
In this case, augmentation may not fail because weather simulation is useless, but because the pretrained model already provides a strong starting point and
adding augmented data to the training dataset may not benefit the model overall, as it introduces a domain shift.

Second, the Rain and Fog Cycle-GAN models are trained using images cropped at size $384\times384$, which is small compared with the original BDD100K size $1280\times720$ and the original Cityscapes size $2048\times1024$. 
Despite speeding up the training process, a small crop size sacrifices stability and performance. The trained Cycle-GAN models are unstable: the presence of white random noise and the unrealistic appearance of rain and fog in the augmented datasets negatively affect the performance of the YOLOv8n model trained on them. 

Third, as discussed in Section \ref{sec:section-rain-gan}, the BDD100K rainy images are mostly captured post-rainfall; therefore, the differences between rainy and clear images appear to be minor. 
Accordingly, the Rain Cycle-GAN generator trained on these post-rainfall images may be unable to capture real rain features well. This effect may undermine the YOLOv8n object-detection performance.

Last, the clean-augmented combined dataset contains 3,000 clean images and 3,000 augmented images, with a clean-to-augmented ratio of 1:1. This may have a negative effect on YOLOv8n performance, 
as a large portion of augmented images may shift the domain to a ``weather-specific'' domain; the model overlearns features from the augmented images while underrepresenting the clean images.

\subsection{Summary}
Overall, the results show that augmentation is most useful when it supplements clean training data instead of replacing it.
Training only on augmented images generally reduced performance on clear and rainy images, likely because the augmented images create a domain gap between the augmented images and the clear images.
In contrast, combined training preserved clear-weather accuracy while adding exposure to adverse-weather appearances.

The biggest improvement on object detection robustness appears in the foggy split, where PBR plus GAN and clean plus PBR plus GAN both outperformed the clean baseline.
The rainy split did not show a clear improvement over clean-only training; however, combined augmentation outperformed augmented-only training while preserving performance close to clear-only training.
These findings support the proposed framework as a useful dataset expansion method, especially when real adverse-weather data are limited, unbalanced, or difficult to collect with controllable and consistent labels.

\section{Future Work}

Future work should first separate the limitations of the augmentation framework from the effects of transfer learning. In this study, YOLOv8n is initialized from pretrained weights,
which may already contain useful features from mild rain and fog. A stronger experiment would be training an object detection model from scratch, using the clear-baseline dataset and augmented datasets. 
Models trained from random initialization should also be compared with the pretrained detectors, so a conclusive result about the effect of transfer learning could be drawn. 

The Cycle-GAN components should also be improved through larger and higher-resolution training. The current Rain and Fog Cycle-GAN models were trained with images cropped at $384\times384$, 
which likely contributed to white noise, over-desaturation, overexposure, and clustered fog artifacts. Future work could use higher-resolution training and larger training domains for the training process. 
For fog, combining Cycle-GAN appearance transfer with a depth-aware atmospheric scattering model (ASM), which is currently a mainstream solution to synthetic fog, may preserve the realistic global style of generated fog while improving physical control over visibility and scene depth~\cite{sakaridisSemanticFoggyScene2018,cordtsCityscapesDatasetSemantic2016}.

Another direction is to improve the rainy target domain. Many BDD100K rainy images appear to show post-rainfall scenes rather than active rainfall, so the Rain Cycle-GAN generator may only learn to generate subtle rain effects.
Future Rain Cycle-GAN training should consider using a separate active rainfall dataset or a combined active rainfall plus rainy dataset for training. 
The generator would therefore learn a clearer target distribution, and the evaluation of depth-aware rain rendering could be more reliable.

Evaluation should be expanded beyond the current BDD100K validation splits. The foggy validation set contains only 13 images, so the observed improvement on foggy scenes is inconclusive.
Future work should test on larger foggy benchmarks. Cross-dataset evaluation would show whether the proposed augmentation improves robustness across different cameras, locations, weather distributions, etc.
Additional downstream tasks, such as image segmentation or depth estimation, could also be tested to produce a study on the effect of data augmentation.

The current clean-plus-augmented training dataset for the YOLOv8n model contains 3,000 clean images and 3,000 augmented images. As discussed in \autoref{sec:section-limitations}, this may lead to overrepresentation or underrepresentation of specific features or domains.
Future work should consider optimizing the clear-to-augmented ratio, so the object detection model balances performance across adverse-weather conditions.

Finally, future work should study the effect of different severity levels on data augmentation. Due to the length of this study, this work does not investigate or compare the effects of different rain severities on data augmentation.
In fact, this is a valuable extension to the research area, as it provides a more detailed and coherent understanding of the effects of severity in adverse-weather data augmentation.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper develops and evaluates a depth-aware data augmentation framework for simulating rain and fog distortions in outdoor RGB driving images. 
The project was motivated by the difficulty of collecting real adverse-weather datasets at scale, the imbalance across weather type and severity, and 
the scarcity of controlled conditions and labels for distortion classification and detection training and systematic robustness testing. 
By applying controllable synthetic weather to BDD100K images, the framework turns an existing clear-weather driving dataset into a controlled adverse-weather dataset,
making it usable for training object detection models.

The proposed rain pipeline combines two complementary ideas. The physics-based renderer provides direct control over rain streak density, opacity, 
apparent depth, mist, and visibility, using Depth Anything V2 to prevent physically inconsistent rain appearance across foreground and background objects. 
The Cycle-GAN model adds global rainy appearance changes, including wet road surfaces, dimmer lighting, and desaturated skies. The hybrid PBR plus 
GAN approach therefore combines global rainy-style appearance with controllable depth-aware rain synthesis. For fog, a Cycle-GAN trained using the Cityscapes 
and Foggy Cityscapes datasets produces foggy BDD100K images with reduced contrast, softened distant objects, and desaturated appearance.

The YOLOv8n evaluation shows mixed but useful results. On clear and rainy validation images, the clean baseline remained strongest, reaching 0.1589 
and 0.1689 mAP50--95, respectively. Augmented-only training generally reduced performance, suggesting that synthetic weather can introduce a domain gap 
when it replaces clean data. However, clean-plus-augmented training stayed close to the clean baseline. For example, clean plus depth-aware PBR reached 
0.1583 on clear images and 0.1655 on rainy images, while clean plus PBR plus GAN reached 0.1580 and 0.1634. These results suggest that weather augmentation 
is more reliable when it supplements clean data rather than replacing it.

The most promising improvement appeared on foggy images. The clean baseline achieved 0.1419 mAP50--95 on the foggy split, while PBR plus GAN reached 
0.2259 and clean plus PBR plus GAN reached 0.1987. This indicates that adverse-weather augmentation may help detectors generalize to low-visibility scenes. 
However, because the foggy validation split contains only 13 images, this result requires further validation on larger datasets.

Overall, the framework shows that depth-aware and data-driven augmentation can create useful controlled weather variation, but its effectiveness depends 
on multiple factors, including training method, data size, and image resolution. Future work should train Cycle-GAN models with larger and higher-resolution datasets, 
improve the rainy target domain with active-rainfall images, combine fog translation with mainstream depth-aware atmospheric scattering models, evaluate on larger foggy and 
cross-dataset benchmarks, compare pretrained and from-scratch object detection models, and study different clean-to-augmented ratios and weather severity levels.
